# Why Do Shopify Stores Get Sued for ADA Accessibility?

In 2024, more than 4,600 ADA website accessibility lawsuits were filed in federal courts across the United States. Ecommerce sites, including Shopify stores, represent a significant share of those targets. If you run a Shopify store, you may be wondering what makes an online business vulnerable to these lawsuits and what specific issues tend to trigger legal action.

The short answer is that the Americans with Disabilities Act requires businesses to provide equal access to their services. When a website effectively locks out people with disabilities, it creates legal exposure. Here is a deeper look at how this works and what actually drives these lawsuits.

## How ADA Title III Reaches Shopify Stores

The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) was signed into law in 1990, long before the internet existed in its current form. Title III of the ADA prohibits discrimination based on disability in places of public accommodation. Courts have increasingly interpreted this language to include websites because they function as the digital equivalent of a physical store.

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.1 Level AA serve as the de facto standard for determining whether a website is accessible. These guidelines cover how websites should handle images, navigation, forms, media, color contrast, and keyboard access. When a Shopify store falls short in these areas, it creates conditions that can lead to a lawsuit.

Shopify itself provides some accessibility tools and themes, but the responsibility ultimately falls on the store owner. Third-party themes, customizations, and apps can all introduce accessibility problems that a plaintiff can point to during litigation.

## What Actually Triggers ADA Lawsuits

Not every accessibility issue leads to a lawsuit. Serial plaintiffs and their attorneys look for stores with clear, documentable barriers that prevent people with disabilities from completing basic tasks like browsing products, adding items to a cart, and checking out.

The most common violations include:

**Missing alternative text on product images.** Screen reader users rely on alt text to understand what an image shows. When product photos lack descriptive alt attributes, visually impaired customers cannot learn about your merchandise.

**Non-keyboard-accessible navigation.** Many users cannot use a mouse and depend on keyboard navigation to move through a site. If your store requires a mouse for essential actions, those customers are locked out.

**Insufficient color contrast.** Text that blends into the background makes content difficult or impossible to read for people with low vision or color blindness.

**Missing form labels and error identification.** Checkout forms that lack properly labeled fields and clear error messages create barriers for users with assistive technology.

**Video content without captions or transcripts.** Auto-playing video with no captions excludes customers who are deaf or hard of hearing.

**Unlabeled buttons and links.** Buttons that say "click here" or links with generic text provide no context to screen reader users trying to navigate the page.

A plaintiff does not need to prove they suffered harm in most jurisdictions. They only need to demonstrate that a barrier existed and that they encountered it or were deterred from trying to access the store.

## Serial Filer Economics Drive Volume

A large portion of ADA website lawsuits come from a small group of plaintiffs and law firms. These serial filers identify stores with accessibility problems, file complaints, and negotiate settlements. The economics favor this approach because:

Settlements often cost less than hiring a lawyer to fight the case. Many small Shopify merchants settle for a few thousand dollars plus an agreement to fix the issues. This predictable outcome makes the strategy attractive to plaintiffs attorneys who handle these cases on contingency.

The process scales easily. Automated tools can scan thousands of Shopify stores in a short time, flagging accessibility violations. Serial filers can cast a wide net and identify hundreds of potential targets with minimal effort.

State courts in New York, Florida, and California have been especially active venues for these lawsuits. Stores with customers in those states face elevated risk regardless of where the business is located.

## Protecting Your Shopify Store

Understanding why lawsuits happen is the first step. Taking action is what matters.

Start by running an accessibility audit on your store. Free tools like WAVE and axe can identify many common issues. For a thorough review, consider hiring a consultant who specializes in WCAG compliance.

Audit your theme and installed apps. Remove anything you do not actively use. Each app you install can introduce new accessibility problems.

Implement alt text policies for your product images. Build this into your workflow so every new product listing includes descriptive alternative text.

Test your checkout flow using only a keyboard. Work through the entire purchase process without touching your mouse or trackpad.

Ensure your theme meets WCAG 2.1 AA standards. If you are using a custom or older theme, consider switching to one built with accessibility in mind.

Set up ongoing monitoring. Accessibility is not a one-time fix. As you add products, change themes, or install new apps, new barriers can appear.

Make accessibility part of your routine. When you launch new landing pages, add products, or run promotions, check that the new content meets the same standards as your existing pages.

The goal is not just to avoid lawsuits. An accessible store reaches more customers, improves the experience for everyone, and demonstrates that you value all shoppers equally.
